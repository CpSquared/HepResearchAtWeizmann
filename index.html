Falafel Seminar A Summary of All of the Results From the World-Leading LZ (LUX-ZEPLIN) Experiment

Speaker: Matthew Szydagis.

Affiliation: Associate Professor at the University at Albany, State University of New York, USA. Homepage.

Seminar starts at: 13:00, Israel time.

Refreshments served at: 12:30, Israel time.

Date: 12 January, Monday.

Venue: Physics library.

Zoom link: Zoom (TBA).

Abstract: The LUX-ZEPLIN (LZ) experiment is primarily designed to search for interactions of dark matter in the form of weakly-interacting massive particles (WIMPs) using a 7 tonne dual-phase xenon time projection chamber. LZ is located at the Sanford Underground Research Facility in Lead, South Dakota, where it has been acquiring science data since 2021. After a combined exposure of 4.2 tonne-years from all science campaigns, LZ placed the most stringent limits on spin-independent WIMP-nucleon cross sections, down to 2.2 x 10^-48 cm^2 for a 40 GeV/c^2 WIMP mass, and, more recently, it has increased its exposure to 5.7 tonne-years and placed world-leading limits on both spin-independent and spin-dependent-neutron WIMP-nucleon cross sections down to 5 GeV/c^2. At the same time, LZ found 4.5-sigma-level evidence of low-energy, coherent elastic scattering from Boron-8 solar neutrinos. This talk will discuss the current status of the LZ experiment and its latest endeavors across many different forms of searches in the fields of dark matter as well as neutrino physics, along with its robust calibrations and powerful simulations, based on the NEST (Noble Element Simulation Technique) software.

Abstract: In this talk, I will discuss my efforts together with my colleague Prof. Cecilia Levy on both LZ-related and unrelated initiatives. In the first half, I will continue discussing the Noble Element Simulation Technique (NEST) from my first talk. This gold-standard sim, now featured in the Particle Data Group (PDG) review, describes light and charge yields of different types of energy depositions in liquid and gas Xe and Ar as functions of energy and electric fields, along with fluctuations, and temporal pulse structures, at an unprecedented degree of precision. NESTâ€™s semi-empirical approach, which grew out of LZ predecessor LUX, has demonstrated incredible postdictive/predictive powers for many other experiments, including ZEPLIN, XENON, and PandaX for calibration and signal-background discrimination, namely, the separation of nuclear recoils from electron recoils. NEST's focus is still the traditional WIMP, however; to search for sub-GeV-mass dark matter, we have developed a new particle detection tech, i.e., the snowball chamber, the topic of the second half. It relies on supercooled water only freezing due to nuclear recoil. Like the bubble/cloud chambers before it, the snowball chamber has incredible promise.

Group Meeting - Gilad Perez Dark Matter Recoherence

Speaker: Chaitanya Paranjape.

Abstract: The local solar gravitational potential forms a basin for ultralight dark matter (ULDM), with discrete energy levels. Even if barely populated, it introduces a new characteristic timescale in DM dynamics. This necessitates a generalization of the notion of coherence time. We find that, at long times, the phenomenon of recoherence emerges, whereby a subcomponent of ULDM exhibits a formally divergent coherence time. The fact that this generalized coherence time can significantly exceed the naive estimate implies an enhanced sensitivity for dark matter searches that accumulate data over extended observation periods. Based on the work, arXiv:2601.00955.
